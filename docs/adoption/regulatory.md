# Regulatory Position: Fair Launch & Commodity Classification

> **Last updated:** March 2026. Regulatory frameworks are evolving. Nothing on this page constitutes legal advice.

## Why Regulatory Classification Matters

Whether a digital asset is classified as a **security** or a **commodity** determines which US agency has jurisdiction, what compliance burdens apply, and which exchanges can list it. Kaspa's design choices -- PoW consensus, fair launch, no premine, no ICO -- place it on the strongest possible path toward commodity classification for any cryptocurrency that is not Bitcoin itself.

## The Howey Test and Fair Launch

Under the [SEC v. W.J. Howey Co. (1946)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/328/293/) framework, an asset is a security if it involves:

1. An **investment of money**
2. In a **common enterprise**
3. With an **expectation of profits**
4. Derived from the **efforts of others**

Most PoS chains with ICOs, premines, or team allocations satisfy all four prongs at launch: investors bought tokens from an identifiable team, expecting the team's development work to increase token value.

**Kaspa's fair launch eliminates the Howey nexus:**

- **No ICO or token sale** -- no "investment of money" in a common enterprise.
- **No premine or team allocation** -- no identifiable party whose efforts drive profit expectations.
- **All tokens distributed through mining** -- acquisition requires work, not investment in a promoter.
- **No foundation controlling supply** -- no VC backstop, no insider token unlock schedules.

This mirrors Bitcoin's regulatory path and is the reason Bitcoin has never been classified as a security.

## SEC "Project Crypto" Framework (November 2025)

In November 2025, SEC Chairman Paul Atkins outlined a token taxonomy under the initiative called ["Project Crypto"](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/speeches-statements/atkins-111225-secs-approach-digital-assets-inside-project-crypto). The framework proposes classifying **"digital commodities / network tokens"** as NOT securities when their value derives from "functional and decentralized" network operation rather than the managerial efforts of a centralized team.

Kaspa's properties align directly with this classification:

- Value derives from network utility (fast, low-fee PoW settlement), not from a team's promises.
- The network is operationally decentralized with multiple independent ASIC manufacturers, no controlling foundation treasury, and community-driven development.

## CLARITY Act (H.R. 3633)

The [CLARITY Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/3633/text) passed the US House of Representatives in **July 2025** with a bipartisan 294-134 vote. Key provisions:

- **Defines "digital commodities"** as blockchain-based fungible assets that are not securities.
- **Grants the CFTC** primary authority over digital commodity spot markets.
- **Establishes criteria** for when a digital asset transitions from a potential security (at initial offering) to a commodity (once sufficiently decentralized and functional).

**Senate status:** Reconciliation pending as of March 2026.

If enacted, the CLARITY Act would formalize the commodity classification path that Kaspa's design already satisfies. A PoW chain with a fair launch, no insider allocations, and decentralized mining fits the "digital commodity" definition more cleanly than virtually any other asset class.

## Comparison: PoW Fair Launch vs. PoS with ICO/Premine

| Property | Kaspa (PoW, fair launch) | Typical PoS chain (ICO/premine) |
|---|---|---|
| Token distribution | 100% through mining | ICO, team allocation, VC rounds |
| Howey test exposure | Minimal -- no investment contract | Significant at launch |
| Insider token unlocks | None | Common (vesting schedules) |
| Foundation treasury | No pre-allocated tokens | Often billions in tokens |
| Commodity classification path | Direct (mirrors Bitcoin) | Requires "sufficient decentralization" argument |
| SEC enforcement risk | Low | Variable -- several PoS tokens have faced SEC action |

> **Important nuance:** Some PoS chains may eventually satisfy decentralization criteria under frameworks like the CLARITY Act. The distinction is that Kaspa never needed to "become" sufficiently decentralized -- it started that way.

## What This Means for Kaspa

1. **Exchange listings:** Regulatory clarity reduces legal risk for exchanges considering spot listings. If KAS is clearly a commodity, exchanges face fewer compliance hurdles.
2. **Institutional adoption:** Institutional investors require regulatory certainty. A commodity classification opens doors to funds, ETPs, and corporate treasury allocation.
3. **Long-term positioning:** As the regulatory environment tightens, assets with clean launch histories will be increasingly differentiated from those facing retroactive securities scrutiny.

## Limitations

- The CLARITY Act has not yet passed the Senate. The regulatory landscape could change.
- SEC enforcement priorities shift between administrations.
- Commodity classification does not exempt an asset from all regulation -- CFTC oversight still applies.
- This analysis focuses on US regulation. Other jurisdictions have different frameworks.
